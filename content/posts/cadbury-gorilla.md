## An Ad That Explains Nothing

In 2007, Cadbury aired a television commercial with the following content: a gorilla, seated at a drum kit, playing Phil Collins's "In the Air Tonight." The gorilla plays it well. With feeling, even. The famous drum fill arrives — one of the most anticipated moments in rock history — and the gorilla commits to it completely.

Then a brief pack shot. Then nothing.

No narrative. No product claim. No explanation of what a gorilla has to do with chocolate. Not even a particularly strong argument that this was an advertisement rather than a fever dream.

It was voted the best British TV ad of all time. Sales of Cadbury Dairy Milk increased by 9%.

## Incongruity Resolution, and Its Absence

Most humor works through a two-stage process called incongruity resolution: something violates expectations, and then the punchline explains the violation in a way that makes sense in retrospect. You laugh at the resolution.

The Cadbury gorilla creates incongruity at every level. Gorillas don't play drums. "In the Air Tonight" is not an advertising song. Nothing about any of this belongs in a chocolate commercial. The brain loads its incongruity-detection systems and waits for the punchline.

The punchline never arrives. There is no resolution. And that is the mechanism.

Psychologists Peter McGraw and Caleb Warren identified what they call the benign violation — something that violates norms but poses no genuine threat. Humor in this case arises not from resolution but from the brain's recognition that the violation is harmless. The gorilla ad is a benign violation that refuses to explain itself, which means the incongruity stays active throughout the viewing. The brain keeps waiting. The gorilla keeps drumming. And the longer the absurdity continues without collapsing, the more pleasurable it becomes.

What tips it into delight rather than confusion is the gorilla's commitment. There's no wink, no acknowledgment that any of this is unusual. The gorilla plays the drums as if this is the most important thing happening anywhere in the world. That total earnestness inside a completely absurd situation is itself the joke — and it's a joke that only gets funnier the longer it holds.

## Low-Involvement Processing

Robert Heath's research on advertising effectiveness identified a phenomenon he called low-involvement processing: ads that don't appear to be trying to persuade are often more effective than ads that do.

When viewers sense they're being sold to, they activate what Friestad and Wright called persuasion knowledge — a set of cognitive defenses that evaluate and resist the message. "I know what you're doing and I've decided whether to believe you." The Cadbury gorilla doesn't look like an ad. It looks like a piece of entertainment that happens to have a Cadbury logo at the end. Persuasion defenses never fully activate.

Because the viewer never mobilizes resistance, the positive emotion generated by the ad attaches freely to the brand. The affect heuristic does the rest: you felt something good; Cadbury was there; now Cadbury is, at some quiet level, associated with that feeling.

The pack shot timing is precise. After 90 seconds of accumulated delight, the brain is highly receptive to whatever appears last. The Cadbury logo arrives into a moment of elevated positive affect and gets tagged to it. That's the entire commercial mechanism — and it required almost no product messaging to execute.

## What Cadbury Got Right

The gorilla demonstrated that joy can be a brand identity in itself, independent of any product claim. Cadbury didn't tell you their chocolate was joyful. They were joyful — inexplicably, absurdly, memorably joyful — for 90 seconds, and trusted the audience to make the connection.

That trust is itself meaningful. An ad that makes no logical argument respects the audience's capacity to receive something strange and emerge feeling good without having it explained to them. Most advertising doesn't trust its audience that far.

Dozens of brands have tried to replicate the formula since 2007. Unexpected animal, beloved song, no product message. Few have matched it, because the formula isn't the thing — the thing is the commitment. The gorilla works because it's completely sincere in its absurdity. The imitators tend to look like they know they're being quirky, which kills the effect immediately.
